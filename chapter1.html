Chapter 1: Computer System

Introduction

A computer is an electronic device derived from the Latin word "computare," which means to calculate. It accepts raw data (input), processes it as per the user’s command, stores it, and outputs meaningful information. Computers follow the IPO (Input-Process-Output) cycle and the GIGO (Garbage In, Garbage Out) principle, meaning the quality of output depends on the quality of input.

Characteristics/Features of Computers

Computers have several features that make them versatile and efficient:

  • Accuracy: Computers are highly accurate, with results being 100% correct unless there’s human error.
  • Speed: Computers work at incredibly high speeds, performing millions or even billions of instructions per second.
  • Storage: Computers have significant storage capabilities, with data stored in units like megabytes (MB), gigabytes (GB), and terabytes (TB).
  • Versatility: They can perform various tasks across different domains.
  • Diligence: Computers can work continuously without getting tired.
  • Automation: Once given instructions, computers perform tasks without the need for continuous input.

Applications/Uses of Computers

Computers are widely used across various fields:

  • Education: Computers enhance learning through interactive graphics and online resources.
  • Communication: Computers enable fast communication via email, chat, video conferencing, etc.
  • Business: Computers are used for data management, accounting, and e-commerce.
  • Engineering & Design: They assist in complex computations and design tasks, such as building structures or electrical circuits.
  • Others: Computers also have applications in science, research, medicine, military, and robotics.

Capabilities and Limitations of Computers

Capabilities/Advantages/Merits/Pros of Computers:

  • High data processing and storage capacity.
  • Consistent speed and accuracy.
  • Ability to solve complex problems in various fields.
  • Facilitates communication and information exchange.

Limitations/Disadvantages/De-merits/Drawback/Cons of Computers:

  • Computers cannot make decisions or think independently.
  • They depend on electricity or batteries to function.
  • Extended use can strain human eyes.
  • They require peripherals and software to operate fully.
  • They cannot provide human-like feedback or draw conclusions.

Generations of Computers

Computers have evolved over several generations, from mechanical devices to modern-day digital machines:

1. First Generation (1946-1958)

  • Used vacuum tubes as their basic electronic components.
  • Large in size, consumed more electricity, and generated a large amount of heat.
  • Unreliable and prone to frequent hardware failure.
  • Machine-level language was used to program these computers.
  • Example: ENIAC, EDVAC, EDSAC, UNIVAC.

2. Second Generation (1959-1964)

  • Used transistors as their basic electronic components.
  • Smaller in size, consumed less electricity, and generated less heat.
  • Less prone to hardware failure and more reliable.
  • Assembly language was used to program these computers.
  • Example: IBM 7000, IBM 1401, IBM 1620, NCR 304, MARK III, ATLAS.

3. Third Generation (1965-1974)

  • Used Integrated Circuits (ICs) as their basic electronic components.
  • Smaller in size, consumed less electricity, and generated less heat.
  • Faster and more reliable than previous generations.
  • Widely used for commercial applications.
  • High-level languages were used to program these computers.
  • Example: PDP-8, PDP-11, ICL 2900 series, IBM 360, IBM 370.

4. Fourth Generation (1975-1990)

  • Used VLSI (Very Large Scale Integrated Circuits) as their main electronic components.
  • Smaller and portable; faster in processing and more reliable.
  • Larger memory, up to GB and TB.
  • Advanced high-level languages were used for programming.
  • Example: Apple Macintosh, IBM PC.

5. Fifth Generation (1990 onwards)

  • Used ULSI (Ultra Large Scale Integrated Circuit) or microprocessors as major components.
  • Have artificial intelligence (AI) and high processing power due to parallel processing.
  • Capable of tasks like image processing, speech recognition, etc.
  • Advanced high-level languages used for programming.

Computer System Overview

A computer system is the combination of hardware, software, and peripherals that work together to process data and generate meaningful output. It follows the IPO (Input-Process-Output) cycle. The architecture defines how the various hardware components interact to perform tasks effectively.

Components of a Computer System

  • Hardware: The physical components like input devices (keyboard, mouse), output devices (monitor, printer), and processing units (CPU, RAM).
  • Software: Programs that tell the hardware how to function, such as the operating system and application software.
  • Data/Information: Raw facts (data) processed to provide meaningful results (information).
  • People/User: The operators who interact with the system and generate commands.
  • Procedure: Instructions and guidelines on how the system should be used.
  • Communication: Data exchange between components and external systems.

Basic Hardware Components of a Computer System

  • Input Unit: Devices that allow data to be entered into the system (e.g., keyboard, mouse).
  • Output Unit: Devices that display or produce the processed information (e.g., monitor, printer).
  • Processing Unit: The central unit responsible for performing computations (e.g., CPU).
  • Storage Unit: Devices that store data for later use (e.g., hard drive, RAM).

Computer System Components

Input Unit

Data and instruction must enter the computer system before any computation can be performed on the supplied data. The input device accepts data and instruction from the user, which are then converted into a suitable form that is accepted by the computer system. The input unit acts as an interface/bridge for communication between the user and the computer system. Below are the functions performed by the input unit:

  • It accepts the list of instructions and data from the outside world.
  • It converts the supplied instructions and data into a computer-acceptable form (binary code).
  • It supplies the converted instructions and data to the computer system for further processing.

Some commonly used input devices include:

  • Keyboard, trackpad, touchscreen, mouse, light pen, scanner, BCR, MICR, OCR, joystick, trackball, etc.

Output Unit

The job of an output unit is the reverse of the input unit. It supplies information and results of computation to the outside world. It consists of devices that translate information processed by the computer into a form that humans can understand. The computer-processed information consists of 0s and 1s, which need to be translated into letters, numbers, special symbols, pictures, etc., that people can understand. Below are the functions performed by the output unit:

  • It accepts the result produced by the computer, which is not understandable to us.
  • It converts the result into a human-understandable form.
  • It supplies the converted results to the outside world.

Commonly used output devices include:

  • Monitor, printers, speakers, plotters, etc.

Types of Output Devices

There are two types of output devices:

  • Hard Copy Output: These outputs are in print form on paper or some other material that can be touched and carried to be shown to others. It is permanent in nature and doesn't require continuous power supply. Examples: Output produced by a printer or plotter.
  • Soft Copy Output: Soft copy output refers to data shown on a display screen or produced by a speaker. It is untouchable and temporary. It only exists while there is a computer or continuous electric supply. Examples: Output produced on a monitor or sound produced by speakers.

Central Processing Unit (CPU)

The CPU is the brain of any computer system. It carries out major calculations and comparisons. It is responsible for activating and controlling the operation of other units of a computer system. A microprocessor is a single chip that performs the function of the CPU in micro-computers. The speed of the microprocessor is measured in Megahertz (MHz) and Gigahertz (GHz). The functions of the CPU (processor) are:

  • To carry out processing.
  • To give commands and coordinate with all other parts of the computer system.
  • To control the sequence of operations in the computer system.
  • To help with memory allocation and control the storage of data and instructions.

Components of CPU

  • Arithmetic Logic Unit (ALU): The ALU performs arithmetic and logical operations, such as addition, subtraction, multiplication, division, and logical operations like AND, OR, and NOT.
  • Control Unit (CU): The CU manages and coordinates the operations of the computer system, interpreting instructions and issuing signals for the other units to execute them.
  • Memory Unit (MU): The memory unit stores data and instructions, either temporarily or permanently. The primary memory stores data temporarily, while the secondary memory stores processed data permanently.

Memory Unit

Primary Memory (Main Memory)

Primary Memory is the memory that stores data and instructions currently being used by the computer. It is faster but usually volatile (except for ROM). It is usually smaller and more expensive than secondary storage. Features of primary memory include:

  • It is not used to store data permanently.
  • It is usually volatile, except for ROM.
  • It is faster than secondary storage.
  • It is typically smaller in capacity.
  • It is made of semiconducting materials.

Computer Memory and Classification

Primary Memory

Primary memory is used to store data and instructions temporarily. It is volatile, meaning data is lost when the power is turned off. There are two main types of primary memory:

1.1 RAM (Random Access Memory)

RAM stores data and instructions temporarily and is volatile. A user can both read from and write to it. It is also known as read-write memory. There are two types of RAM:

  • SRAM (Static Random Access Memory): Made up of transistors, it does not require refreshing to retain its content, making it faster but more power-consuming.
  • DRAM (Dynamic Random Access Memory): Made up of capacitors, it needs periodic refreshing to retain its content. It is slower than SRAM but consumes less power and is less expensive.

1.2 ROM (Read Only Memory)

ROM is non-volatile memory used to store data permanently. Data and instructions in ROM remain intact even after power is turned off. The program stored in ROM is called firmware and is responsible for booting the computer. There are several types of ROM:

  • PROM (Programmable Read Only Memory): A blank chip that can only be written once using a special machine. Once written, it cannot be modified.
  • EPROM (Erasable Programmable Read Only Memory): A chip that can be reprogrammed after erasing its content using ultraviolet light.
  • EEPROM (Electrically Erasable Programmable Read Only Memory): A chip that can be erased and reprogrammed repeatedly with electric pulses, without needing to be removed from the system.

Secondary Memory

Secondary memory is used to store data permanently for future reference. Unlike primary memory, it is non-volatile and retains data even when the power is turned off. Some commonly used secondary storage types include:

2.1 Magnetic Storage

In magnetic storage, data is stored in the form of magnetic spots. A magnetic material coating, such as ferric oxide, is used to store data on a disk. Examples of magnetic storage media include:

  • Hard Disk: A sealed storage device made up of multiple magnetic disks (platters) mounted on a spindle. It is used to store large volumes of data permanently. It consists of tracks and sectors to organize the data.
  • Floppy Disk: A flat, round disk coated with magnetic material, used for both reading and writing data. It has small storage compared to hard disks.

2.2 Optical Storage

Optical storage uses laser beams to read and write data. It is also non-volatile and widely used for storing large amounts of data. Some examples include:

  • CD-ROM (Compact Disk - Read Only Memory): A storage medium that can hold up to 700 MB of data, typically used for storing software, audio, and video content.
  • DVD (Digital Versatile Disk): A type of optical storage that can store much larger amounts of data than a CD, making it ideal for video and large files.

2.3 Flash Memory

Flash memory is a non-volatile, erasable, and programmable solid-state memory made from semiconductor chips. It is known for its speed and can be reprogrammed without special equipment. Examples include memory cards and USB drives.

Classification of Computers Based on Size

Computers are classified based on their size, processing power, and storage capacity. The main categories include:

Supercomputer

Supercomputers are the fastest and most powerful computers, typically used for specialized tasks such as weather forecasting, scientific simulations, and rocket launching. They are very large, expensive, and not commercially used.

Mainframe Computer

Mainframe computers are large and fast, often used by corporations and government agencies for tasks like census data processing and large-scale record keeping. They have high processing power and large storage capacity.

Mini Computer

Mini computers are smaller than mainframes but still powerful, handling more input and output than microcomputers. They are used in medium-sized businesses, industries, and universities.

Micro Computer

Microcomputers are the smallest type of computer, using microprocessors as the central processing unit. They are low-cost and commonly used in homes, schools, and businesses. Examples include:

  • Desktop: A personal computer typically placed on a desk, known for its versatility in various applications.
  • Laptop: A portable computer that can be carried easily. It is widely used by travelers and business people.
  • Palmtop/Handheld: The most portable and smallest computers, such as smartphones, PDAs, and tablets, which can fit in the palm of a hand.
